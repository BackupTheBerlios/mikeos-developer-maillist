> > Still leaves SIN, COS and TAN though.

Heh, you say that like it's a problem. Seven terms of the taylor series
is more than enough for an accurate result of sin/cos/tan within, I'd
say, -pi <-> +pi. The best bit, for those that don't already know, is
that it uses trivial algebra: factorial (multiplication) exponentation
(multiplication), division and + and -. In terms of floating point nums,
with some slight mods to the formula we could calculate the result, say,
times (2^12-1) and then just interpret it as a floating point number in
manipulation (ie: for printing we just throw out a . after the the first
4 bits) - or something to that effect.

In fact, I am aware of some *very* handy routines for such complex
maths, such as the famous FastInvSqrt with it's magic number that
requires almost zero calculation effort for good approximations and how
to sum up the number of ones in a number using only bitwise operations
and modular addition [which can be done bitwise anyway]. The obscure
world of digital hardware algorithms awaits :P

Oh, right, when I get a moment later this week, I'll throw together a
simple maths lib (or just port one) with [some of] the abovementioned
goodies. Who knows, we may even get a working raycaster in BASIC soon ;)
