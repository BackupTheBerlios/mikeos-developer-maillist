It's not a case of whether you 'need' Octal, it's a case of whether you
think it would be more elegant to use it.

It's great if you don't want to work with fiddly binary numbers but want to
work in one byte units. For instance:

10o = 1000b
20o = 10000b
30o = 11000b
40o = 100000b

10o holds all the 8-bits in a byte, then you have the lower half (00-04o)
and the higher half (05-09o). As you graduate to reference more bytes, it
follows a linear pattern (though you could always use hex in place of the
even multiples at the loss of clarity [i.e. 20o & 40o])

You could do this using binary, but Octal ensures you handle things in a
more elegant way and makes it easier to visualise what is happening. If you
want to work with 2 bytes at a time, hex is a great option because you have
01h-07h as the first byte and 08h-0Fh as the second. I personally prefer
Octal because it works with bytes in a way that makes sense. But I'm using
this as a case in point to say that there is no 'need' for any technique
over another. It's simply a decision of what you feel may be clearer to new
users.
